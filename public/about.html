Timeline.wang is an interactive timeline created by Ainsley Rutterford and James O'Reilly as part of the third year computer science unit "Web Technologies" at the University of Bristol. The name for the website was inspired by our flatmate and close friend Justin Wang (in retrospect, this domain name was perhaps not the most sensible choice).

Motivation

While websites such as Google and Wikipedia provide essential utilities to the public, the websites which are most memorable to us are those which present unique and captivating experiences. These websites often cause us to view something with a fresh perspective. We want each user that visits this website to be filled with a sense of intrigue and curiosity as they experience modern human history in an immersive and interactive way.

We believe in the importance of history as a reminder of both our past failures and past successes, providing direction and hope as we are faced with the challenges of the 21st century. Constructing timelines allow us to see how humanity has progressed, not only in general, but within specific fields such as medicine, space exploration and computer science.

Design

When designing this website, we decided to avoid using frameworks wherever possible. We viewed this as an opportunity to learn and preferred to not abstract away anything that we did not already fully understand. It is rewarding to know that every element of this website was designed and implemented by us alone. We may use frameworks going forward now that we have a full understanding of the details that frameworks abstract.

In the Profile page, each user is assigned an avatar on page refresh. We wanted to create avatars that fit with the philosophy of the website. We designed old geometry constructions, edited to look as though they were drawn in ink on an academic paper. To us, these avatars symbolise curiosity and the boundless pursuit of knowledge.

Timeline

For the timeline page, we wanted to create the illusion of moving through time. To achieve this, each point in time can be given a position in three dimensional space, and the computer screen effectively displays the view of a pinhole camera which is moving through this space. Each contribution is then placed at a point in three dimensional space based on its historical date. The user should be able to control the speed and direction with which they move through this space, and therefore the speed and direction with which they move through time. The timeline is written in pure JavaScript, making use of the HTML5 <canvas> element. The only extrenal library used is momentjs.

Preliminaries

Pinhole Camera Model: The pinhole camera model describes the mathematical relationship between the coordinates of a point in three-dimensional space and its projection onto the image plane of an ideal pinhole camera, where the camera aperture is described as a point and no lenses are used to focus light.

Serialising Dates: In order to easily compare different points in time, the historical dates were serialised according to the number of days that had passed since the beginning of the Roman calendar. When the user submits a contribution, the historical date is first serialised before the contribution is added to the database. Therefore all calculations and comparisons regarding dates can be done using positive integers. In the calculations that follow, each date is an integer value. Serialising was performed using the momentjs JavaScript library.

Defining a three-dimensional coordinate system

The \(X\) and \(Y\)-axes of the three dimensional space are the usual \(X\) and \(Y\)-axes as expected. It is important to note that these axes are not the same as the \(X\) and \(Y\)-axes of the computer screen, which will be denoted by lower case \(x\) and \(y\), although they are related. Time is then defined along the \(Z\)-axis. Originally, the \(Z\)-axis was defined with the earliest contribution at \(Z = 0\) and the latest contribution at \(z = 100\). However, as the camera is initially positioned at \(Z = 0\), the user would not be able to see the first contribution. To account for this, the \(Z\)-position of each contribution was offset by a value \(Z_{offset}\). The \(Z\)-axis therefore begins at \(Z = 0\), with earliest contribution at \(Z = Z_{offset}\) and the latest contribution at \(100 + Z_{offset}\).

Caclulating the \(X\), \(Y\), and \(Z\)-position of each conribution

Now that the coordinate space has been defined, the position of each contribution in that coordinate space can be calculated using the serialised historical date. Let \(S_{earliest}\) be the serialised date of the earliest contribution, let \(S_{latest}\) be the serialised date of the latest contribution, and let \(S_{current}\) be the serialised date of the current contribution whose \(Z\)-position we are calculating. The \(Z\)-position is then calculated as follows: $$ Z_{current} = {\frac{S_{current} - S_{earliest}}{S_{latest} - S_{earliest}}}\cdot{100} + Z_{offset}$$ which gives $$ Z_{offset} \leq Z_{current} \leq Z_{offset} + 100 $$ We now have the \(Z\)-position of each contribution. However, as the camera moves through this space, each contribution will be exactly in front of the next contribution as they share the same \(X\) and \(X\)-positions. This also means that the contributions would pass by the camera in the center of the screen. To add to the experience, the contributions should pass the camera at different \(X\) and \(Y\)-positions. Each contribution was therefore randomly assigned an \(X\) and \(Y\)-position between \(-10\) and \(10\).

Three-Dimensional Projection

The exact position of each contribution in the 3-D space is now known. Next we must define how this 3-D position relates to the position of the contribution on the 2-D screen. 3-D projection is a method of mapping three-dimensional points to a two-dimensional plane. Let \(X_{relative}\), \(Y_{relative}\), and \(Z_{relative}\) be the relative position of any point to the camera. Let \(f\) be the focal length, which represents the distance between the camera and the 2-D screen in 3-D space. Let \(x_{screen}\) and \(y_{screen}\) be the position of a given contribution on the two dimensional screen. Note that for the screen, the \(xy\)-coordinate system is centered at the top left corner, and to center the camera on the screen, the 2-D position is translated by \((x_{center}, y_{center})\) where $$ x_{center} = \frac{ScreenWidth}{2} $$ and $$ y_{center} = \frac{ScreenHeight}{2} $$ The final 2-D screen position for a contribution is then given by \((x_{screen}, y_{screen})\), where $$ x_{screen} = f + \frac{X_{relative}}{Z_{relative}} + x_{center} $$ and $$ y_{screen} = f + \frac{Y_{relative}}{Z_{relative}} + y_{center} $$

Scaling

We now have the position of each contribution on the screen. What remains is to scale these contributions based on their distance from the camera. Naturally, the size of each contribution will be inversely proportional to its relative \(Z\)-position, as elements that are further away in 3-D space (and therefore also in time) should appear smaller on the screen. The size of each contribution is therfore given by $$ size = scale \times \frac{1}{Z_{relative}} $$ where \(scale\) can be defined arbitrarily based on preference. This allows us to scale each element of a contribution, creating the illusion of depth on the screen. The contribution's opacity value is also defined to be inversely proportional to \(Z_{relative}\), so that objects that are further away will fade into the distance. A coloured radial gradient is then applied the the background to enhance the illusion of depth.

Displaying the Current Date

To retrieve the date we must calculate the current serialised date corresponding to the \(Z\)-position of the camera. This is done by rearranging the equation used to calculate the \(Z\)-position of a contribution: $$ S_{camera} = (\frac{Z_{camera} - Z_{offset}}{100})\cdot(S_{latest} - S_{earliest}) + S_{earliest}$$ This serialised date is then converted back to a standard date format and presented to the user. This date will update as the camera moves along the \(Z-axis\).
